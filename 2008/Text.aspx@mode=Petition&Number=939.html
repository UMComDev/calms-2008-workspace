Establish Case Review Panel (80939-JA-NonDis-!)

    The 2008 General Conference shall establish a Case Review Panel to examine any cases since 1980 where pastors felt they had been treated unfairly in order to identify breakdowns in how our administrative and judicial processes worked in their cases.  
   The Review Panel shall be made up of three retired clergy and four laity (two lawyers, one personnel expert, and one at large member) who have not been related to any of the cases acted upon by cabinets, committees, courts, boards or councils since 1980, and that their election be by the General Conference after nomination from the floor of General Conference so that the panel can be convened to organize and begin its work by the end of July, 2008.  Ad interim replacements shall be made by the General Council on Finance and Administration.  
    Their review shall be primarily directed to the kinds of mistakes made so that any future legislation relating to the complaint processes will at least minimize those mistakes and that those mistakes be identified for the Council of Bishops, General Council on Finance and Administration, General Board of Higher Education and Ministry Division of Ordained Ministry, General Board of Global Ministries. General Commission on the Role and Status of Women, the Judicial Council, and the 2012 General Conference.  
    Secondarily, if any of the cases were egregious in their handling, the panel shall recommend actions to the appropriate Annual Conferences that could make those situations "whole."
    Expenses for the panel shall be covered by General Council on Finance and Administration, but the expenses related to those bringing their cases shall be covered by those people, their advocates, and/or their family seeking relief.
    Hearings of the panel shall be held in all five United States Jurisdictions in places as central in those areas as possible.

Rationale

I have seen good pastors destroyed by cruel, thoughtless, and incompetent handling of complaints over the last quarter century.  The procedures used have been unfair.  The judging bodies have largely been more interested in supporting the position of the bishop than they were in finding justice.  Even the Judicial Council...
